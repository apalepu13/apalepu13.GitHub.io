I am a third year PhD student at Harvard-MIT Health Sciences & Technology under Andrew Beam and am a member of the Beam Lab at the Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health .

Generally, I am interested in self-supervised learning, vision models, and language models, especially as these relate to the medical domain. In my current work, I aim to characterize and improve contrastive text-image models like the CLIP architecture. In particular, I have been exploring regularization, robustness to spurious correlations, and zero-shot classification techniques for these models.

Previously, I received my M.S.E. and B.S. degrees in Biomedical Engineering from Johns Hopkins University. During that time, I collaborated with Johns Hopkins faculty, clinicians, and hospital administrators on a diverse set of Machine Learning for Healthcare projects, including epilepsy localization, traumatic brain injury prognosis, "precision" trans-arterial embolization, and surgical instrument tracking.

2023

TIER: Text-Image Entropy Regularization for medical CLIP-style models
We develop a regularization method for CLIP-style models which computes pairwise image-patch and text-similarity scores and uses entropy to penalize the model if these similarities are uniform. This regularization encourages text tokens to only describe a few of image patches and each image patch to only correspond to only a few text tokens.
Anil Palepu, Andrew Beam
Conference on Health, Inference and Learning 2023

Self-Supervision on Images and Text Reduces Reliance on Visual Shortcut Features
We demonstrate through synthetically-generated watermark shortcuts on chest x-rays that supervised CNNs are heavily reliant and unable to unlearn shortcuts, and self-supervision with text helps to reduce this reliance.
